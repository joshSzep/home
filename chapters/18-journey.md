# Chapter 18 - Journey

Year 13. Antarctica.

Elena Torres, Mayor of McMurdo District, sits in her office at dawn.

Empty office. No staff. No petitioners. No governance. Just: habit.

She arrives every morning. Unlocks the door. Turns on lights (when power works). Sits at her desk. Opens files no one needs. Maintains records no one will read.

Why?

Because someone should.

Because bearing witness matters even when nothing matters.

Wei understood this. Wrote to Elena Year 10, shortly before her death:

*You govern with dignity even when there's nothing to govern. This is wisdom. Not governing outcomes—governing yourself. Being present in your role until the end. This is enough.*

Elena never met Wei. But the philosopher became, strangely, one of her most important teachers. Through letters. Through Kenji's stories. Through the simple act of living with intention until natural death.

Elena is sixty-seven years old. Two years until the asteroid. Governing nothing but still governing.

Present until the end.

###

Morning routine: check systems.

Power: intermittent. Backup generators failing. Solar arrays—David and Amara's work—still functioning. Barely. Two more years maybe.

Water: reclamation systems degrading. Clean water for two years. Maybe.

Food: greenhouse yields declining. Supplements from storage. Enough for two years. Probably.

Population: 1,847 people. Down from 3,000 Year 1. Some left (went where?). Some died. Some just... stopped registering.

Elena updates the records. Types carefully. Dates everything. Includes weather (minus-forty Celsius, wind thirty knots), observations (three more buildings abandoned this week).

She saves the file. For whom? No one. Just: someone should document this. Someone should say: we were here. We persisted. We mattered.

Even if we didn't. Even if we don't. We tried.

###

Midmorning, Kenji arrives with tea.

Her son. Thirty-eight years old. Returned to Antarctica Year 10, stayed. Works with her now. Not governing—bearing witness together.

They've healed, these past years. The distance. The grief. The different ways of coping. All healed. Or: accepted. Same thing, maybe.

"How's the district?" Kenji asks.

"Failing. Gracefully."

"Is there graceful failing?"

"We're proving there is. Most systems down. But people help each other. Share resources. Form communities. No central authority needed anymore. They're doing it themselves."

"So your job's obsolete."

"Was always obsolete. Post-detection, what's a mayor? Symbolic only. Theater of governance. But: the theater mattered. Gave people structure, familiarity, sense that someone was witnessing."

"And now?"

"Now they don't need the theater. They've learned to be present without it. Which means: I succeeded. Or failed. Depending on perspective."

Kenji smiles. "Wei would say both are true."

"Wei was wise."

"You would have liked her."

"I did like her. Even though we never met. Love doesn't require presence. Just: connection."

###

Weeks later, Zara's letter arrives.

*Mayor Torres,*

*I'm Amara's sister. She invited us to Antarctica. We're coming—me, my husband Kwame, our children Maya (14) and Kofi (11). Should arrive in months if transport holds.*

*Amara says you've maintained governance with dignity to the end. That you kept showing up when others stopped. That you still bear witness.*

*Thank you for that. Someone should. Someone should say: we were here.*

*Looking forward to meeting you.*

*With respect,  
Zara Okafor-Mensah*

Elena reads the letter three times. Grateful.

Family coming. Sisters reuniting. Children—last generation. All converging on Antarctica for the end.

This is what governance becomes: welcoming people home. Making space for final gatherings. Bearing witness to love persisting.

She writes back:

*Zara,*

*Welcome. There's room here. For all of you. For everyone who comes.*

*I'll help coordinate housing, resources, welcome. Not governance—just: making space for people to be together at the end.*

*Your children will meet others. Small community. They'll learn, play, be. Then: together.*

*See you soon.*

*With warmth,  
Elena Torres*

###

Year 14. Lagos to Antarctica.

Zara Okafor-Mensah stands on the deck of the transport ship with her family—Kwame beside her, Maya (sixteen) at the rail, Kofi (twelve) exploring every corner.

Behind them: Lagos. The city disappearing into haze. Home. History. Everything that shaped them.

Ahead: Antarctica. The megacity. Amara. Family. Final gathering.

One year until the asteroid. Twelve months until everyone dies together.

Zara holds Kwame's hand. They've been married nineteen years. Still choosing each other. Still here.

"Are we doing the right thing?" Kwame asks quietly.

"Leaving Lagos? Going to Amara? Yes. We need family. The children need to meet their aunt. We need to face this together."

"I mean: everything. Having them. Raising them. Bringing them to the end."

Zara looks at Maya and Kofi. Her children. Her defiant choice. Her best work.

"Yes," she says firmly. "Every second. Every choice. Yes."

###

The voyage takes three weeks.

Transport ship barely functioning. Ancient vessel, maintained by crew who know it's the last run. Everything's the last now. Last voyage. Last maintenance. Last everything.

But: it moves. Slowly. South through failing oceans.

Maya spends hours at the rail. Watching water. She's sixteen—brilliant, beautiful, mature beyond her years because circumstances demanded it. She'll never be twenty. Never finish growing. Never have the life Zara dreamed for her.

But: she's here. She exists. That's everything.

"Mama," Maya says one evening. "Tell me about Aunt Amara."

"What do you want to know?"

"Why did she disappear? After detection? Why did she stop calling?"

"She was scared. Hiding. She thought isolation would protect her from pain. It didn't—just delayed it."

"Are you angry at her?"

"I was. For years. But Wei taught me about impermanence, about accepting what is. Amara did what she needed to survive. Now she's ready to be sisters again. That's what matters."

"Do you think she'll like us?"

"She'll love you. How could she not? You're extraordinary."

Maya smiles—small, sad. "I won't have children. My line ends with me."

"I know, baby."

"Are you sorry? That you had us? Knowing we'd die young?"

This question again. Different ages. Different contexts. Same terror underneath: did I matter?

"No," Zara says firmly. "Never. Not once. You and Kofi are the best thing I ever did. You made my life matter. I made yours matter. That's enough."

"Even though it's brief?"

"Especially because it's brief. Everything's brief. The asteroid just makes it visible. But love is real. You're real. That's everything."

###

Kofi makes friends with everyone.

The crew. Other passengers. Even the ship's cat—ancient, scarred, persistent.

Zara watches him charm his way through the ship and feels overwhelming love. He's bright, curious, alive. Never knew a world before the asteroid. Accepts ending more easily because it's all he's ever known.

"Papa," Kofi says one evening. "Will Antarctica have penguins?"

"Some," Kwame answers. "Not many left. But some."

"Can I see them?"

"Yes. We'll find them."

"How long do we have? There?"

Kwame and Zara exchange glances. They've never hidden the truth.

"One year," Zara says. "Twelve months. Enough time to see everything. Meet everyone. Be together."

"Then we die."

"Then we die. Together. All of us."

"I'm not scared," Kofi says. "I'm with you."

Zara pulls him close. "We're with you too. Always. Until the end. Then: still together."

###

Week two, storm hits.

Violent. Unexpected. Ship rocks dangerously. Crew scrambling. Passengers terrified.

Zara gathers her family in their cabin. Holds Maya and Kofi. Kwame holds all of them.

"Together," Zara says. "We're together. That's what matters."

The storm rages. Ship groans. Water breaches lower decks.

For hours, they think: this is it. We die now. Not asteroid. Storm. Different ending. Wrong ending.

But: the ship holds. The crew succeeds. The storm passes.

Dawn breaks. Battered ship continues south.

Afterward, Maya says: "I thought we'd die. Apart from Aunt Amara. Apart from everyone."

"We didn't. We're still here."

"But we could have. Everything's dangerous now."

"It always was," Zara says gently. "Life was always fragile. The asteroid just makes it visible. But we survived. We're continuing. That's everything."

###

Week three. Antarctica appears on horizon.

White. Vast. Impossible. The megacity rising from ice—testament to human ambition, human engineering, human belief that forever was possible.

Forever lasted fifty years. Then: fifteen more. Then: done.

But present now. Real now. Beautiful now.

Maya gasps. "It's huge."

"They built it to last ten thousand years," Zara says. "It lasted sixty-five. Not forever. But: real. The attempt mattered."

"Was it worth building? If it doesn't last?"

"The building was real. The people who built it were real. Yes. Worth it."

The ship moves toward dock. McMurdo District growing larger. Ice and architecture and human persistence.

Zara's heart pounds. Amara. After all these years. Finally.

###

The dock is small. Frozen. Few people waiting.

And there—standing wrapped in thermal coat, face weathered by years and grief and work: Amara.

Zara sees her sister and everything breaks open.

They run to each other. Awkward on ice. Crying before they even touch. Embrace desperately.

"I'm sorry," Amara says. "I'm so sorry. I wasted so much time."

"You're here now. That's enough."

"I missed you. I missed everything."

"I'm here. We're here. Look—"

Zara pulls back. Gestures to her family.

"This is Kwame. My husband. And Maya. And Kofi. Your family."

Amara looks at them. Really looks. Sees niece and nephew for first time. Sixteen and twelve. Beautiful. Doomed. Here.

"Hi," Maya says softly. "I'm Maya. I'm so glad to finally meet you."

"I'm Kofi. Do you have penguins?"

Amara laughs—surprised by joy through tears. "Some. Yes. I'll show you."

She pulls Maya into a hug. Then Kofi. Then Kwame. Gathering them all. Family. Finally complete.

Behind Amara: David.

"This is David," Amara says. "My husband. Partner. Everything."

David nods to Zara. "Thank you for coming. We all need each other now."

"Thank you for having us."

They stand on the dock—six people. Two sisters. Two husbands. Two children. Family converged. Facing ending together.

"Come," Amara says. "Let's go home."

###

That night, after children sleep, sisters sit alone.

Small living room. Tea. Fifteen years of distance to process.

"Tell me about them," Amara says. "Maya and Kofi. Tell me everything."

So Zara tells her. Maya's birth right after detection—terror and joy tangled. Kofi's birth Year 1—defiant choice. Growing up knowing. Being honest. Teaching them love, presence, courage. Wei's influence. Kwame's steadiness. The choice every day to love fiercely despite knowing.

"You're so brave," Amara says. "I couldn't do it. I hid. You lived."

"You're here now. Working. Witnessing. That's brave too."

"I wasted ten years. Withdrew from you. From David. From everyone."

"But you came back. Reconnected. Invited us here. That's everything."

Amara cries. "I missed their whole childhoods. I'll never get that back."

"No. But you get one year. That's real. That's something."

"Is it enough?"

"Has to be. What else is there?"

They sit in silence. Sisters who took different paths. Both arriving here. Both present now.

"Thank you for coming," Amara says.

"Nothing to forgive. You did what you needed to survive. I did what I needed. Now we're together. That's what matters."

"One year."

"One year. With family. Facing it together."

###

Next morning, Amara shows them the megacity.

All six together. Walking through McMurdo District. Maya and Kofi amazed—they've only known Lagos, coastal chaos, tropical heat. This: ice, order, architecture of ambition.

"They built this thinking it would last forever," Amara says. "Ten thousand years. Civilization's southern anchor."

"How long did it last?" Maya asks.

"Sixty-five years. Not forever. But: real. Beautiful. Attempt that mattered."

They visit the nature preserve—artificial ecosystem maintaining Earth species. Kofi sees penguins—three of them, last survivors. He's entranced.

"They don't know," Kofi says. "Do they? About the asteroid?"

"No," Amara says. "They're just being penguins. Someone has to care for them until the end. That someone is us."

"That's kind," Maya says. "Witnessing their being. Honoring it. Even if it ends."

They visit the solar arrays. David shows Maya the systems. She understands immediately—brilliant like her mother, curious like her father.

"These will function until impact?" Maya asks.

"Yes," David says. "Maybe few seconds longer. They don't know they're dying. They'll just: work. Until they can't."

Kofi climbs on the lower platforms—supervised, careful. He laughs. Joy in present moment. Twelve years old. One year left. Still playing.

Zara watches him and feels everything at once. Grief. Love. Gratitude. Terror. Acceptance.

Wei taught her: feel it all. Don't choose. Just: be present with everything.

So she is.

###

That evening, they meet Elena and Kenji.

The mayor and her son. Still bearing witness. Still showing up. Dignified to the end.

"Welcome," Elena says to Zara's family. "I'm glad you made it. We need family here. We need love here. We need each other."

Maya asks Elena: "Why do you still govern? If there's nothing to govern?"

"Because someone should witness. Someone should document. Someone should say: we were here. We mattered. That someone is me."

"Is that enough?"

"Has to be. What else is there?"

Kenji talks with Maya about Wei. The teaching. The impermanence. The presence. Maya listens intently—fellow student across generations.

"She taught you well," Maya says. "Through my mother, through time. The teaching continues."

"For one more year," Kenji says. "Then: silence."

"But present now. Real now. Mattering now."

"Exactly. You understand."

They eat together—Elena, Kenji, Amara, David, Zara, Kwame, Maya, Kofi. Ibrahim joins them—the former fighter, faithless now but not angry. Learning to live with uncertainty.

Eight people around one table. Different paths. Same destination. Same insistence on dignity.

"To family," Amara says, raising her glass. "To love persisting. To presence."

"To presence," they echo.

###

That night, Elena updates her records:

*Year 14, Day 312.*

*Zara Okafor-Mensah arrived with family. Amara's sister. Two children—Maya (16) and Kofi (12). Last generation. Beautiful. Doomed. Here.*

*Sisters reunited after fifteen years distance. Love persisting despite everything. This is what matters at the end. Not governance. Not systems. Just: love.*

*One year left. I'll continue bearing witness. Recording who's here. How we persist. How we love.*

*Not for future. There is no future. Just: because someone should say: we were here. We mattered. We maintained dignity to the end.*

*One year left. Then: together with everyone.*

*That's enough.*

She closes the journal. Sits in darkness.

Outside, civilization contracts to its final form. Inside, families gather.

One year left.

One year to be present.

One year to love fiercely.

One year to witness the end with dignity.

Everyone converging.

Everyone together.

Everyone preparing without being ready.

Present.

Always present.

That's everything.

That's enough.

The darkness holds them all.

And in the darkness: light.

Not saving light. Not eternal light.

Just: present light.

Love light.

Family light.

Enough light.

For one more year.

Then—

But not yet.

Present now.

Always present now.
